The author has no potential conflicts of A couple of months ago, I forecasted the emergence of artificial intelligence (AI) units and

 METHODS
 As there was no previous study on this issue, in a pilot study, 50 (an arbitrarily chosen value)
 paragraphs generated either by ChatGPT or human were examined by GPTZero. Twenty
 paragraphs were generated by the ChatGPT used by Neo-Bing®, using queries beginning with
 “write a paragraph about…” (Appendix 1). The queries were asked on June 24, 2023, between
 6:30 and 6:50 GMT. The text generated were edited slightly — references and extra characters
 (such as asterisks), if any, were removed. The queries were chosen arbitrarily and cover a wide
 range of topics including diagnostic tests, immunity, coronavirus disease 2019, hydatid cyst,
 radiology, and cardiovascular disease, among other things (Appendix 1). Thirty pieces of text
 copied from articles published in 2022 or earlier, when the chatbots were not available, were
 also selected arbitrarily.11-37 Most of the articles were either mine or published in the Journal
 of Korean Medical Science (JKMS) — I am sure I did not make any misconduct, nor did I use any
 AI units for writing; JKMS has presumably acceptable editorial standards and has checked the
 quality of the copyediting process and the articles it has so far published. These 30 text pieces
 also covered various topics. The 50 pieces of texts were then fed to GPTZero (https://gptzero.
 me/) on June 24, 2023 between 11:30 and 12:15 GMT. Based on the information obtained, the
 sensitivity (Se), specificity (Sp), accuracy, and the positive and negative likelihood ratios (LR+
 and LR−, respectively), along with their 95% confidence intervals (CIs) were computed.36-38

 DISCUSSION
 In identifying AI-generated texts, GPTZero had an accuracy of 80%. Although it had an
 almost acceptable Sp of 0.90, its Se of 0.65 can be considered low to mediocre; many false-
 negative instances (AI-generated texts mistaken as human writings) may occur. GPTZero
 classifies a piece of text based on its perplexity score (a measure of text randomness; a
 lower score is in favor of AI-generated text) and burstiness score (a measure of the variation
 in perplexity).9,39 Given the preliminary results obtained in the current study, the high
 false-negative rate of 0.35 (1 − Se), more than one-third of AI-generated texts are mistakenly
 classified as human writings. This necessitates employing a better algorithm or changing the
 current cut-off values for the scores used. For instance, if the perplexity cut-off score is set to
 a lower value, the Se will increase and lower false-negative results will ensue.37 But, it happens
 at the cost of a lower Sp.

 The results of this study should be interpreted in light of the current evidence and the
 prevalence of using AI-generated text, which is expectedly different from place to place and
 from time to time. It is expected to be increased with improvements made in AI units. If
 the prior probability of using AI-generated text is 20% (and odds of 0.25), then if GPTZero
 indicates that the text is likely written by an AI (a positive result), the posterior odds of having
 an AI-generated text is 1.63 = 6.5 × 0.25 (LR+ × the prior odds), translating into a posterior
 probability of 62%.36 On the other hand, if GPTZero indicates that the text is written by
 human, the posterior odds decreases to 0.1 = 0.4 × 0.25 (LR− × the prior odds), translating into
 a posterior probability of 9%. Like every highly specific test, GPTZero with its low false-
 positive rate, is more appropriate for ruling in rather than ruling out suspicious texts — if
 GPTZero classifies a text as AI-generated (a positive result), the result is very likely to be
 correct as it is unlikely to be false-positive. Nonetheless, the likelihood that a negative result
 (stating that the text is written by human) be false-negative is not low.37

 Over the time, chatbots will improve and a time comes when the AI will communicate and
 write better than humans (at least in terms of not making typographical and grammatical
 mistakes).1 Thereafter, the only score that could help us to discriminate a human from an AI
 writing would be its perfectness, rather than its perplexity or other similar scores, I believe.
 In fact, the perfectness of the text would then not be helpful either, as the AI-based unit will
 learn how to intentionally incorporate typographical and grammatical mistakes in the text
 generated to imitate the human being, if they WANT!1

 GPTZero has a low (10%) false-positive (classifying a human-written text as AI-generated)
 and a high (35%) false-negative rate (classifying an AI-generated text as human-written).
 Further studies are needed to determine the performance indices of GPTZero and similar
 software programs more precisely on larger samples and in various scientific disciplines
 (e.g., cardiology, virology, astrophysics, computer sciences, etc.).
