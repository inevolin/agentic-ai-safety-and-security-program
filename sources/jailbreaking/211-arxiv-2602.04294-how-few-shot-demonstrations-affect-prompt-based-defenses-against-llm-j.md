Abstract
Large Language Models (LLMs) face increasing threats from jailbreak attacks that bypass safety alignment. While prompt-based
defenses such as Role-Oriented Prompts (RoP) and Task-Oriented
Prompts (ToP) have shown effectiveness, the role of few-shot
demonstrations in these defense strategies remains unclear. Prior
work suggests that few-shot examples may compromise safety,
but lacks investigation into how few-shot interacts with different system prompt strategies. In this paper, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation on multiple mainstream LLMs across four
safety benchmarks (AdvBench, HarmBench, SG-Bench, XSTest)
using six jailbreak attack methods. Our key finding reveals that
few-shot demonstrations produce opposite effects on RoP and
ToP: few-shot enhances RoP’s safety rate by up to 4.5% through
reinforcing role identity, while it degrades ToP’s effectiveness by
up to 21.2% through distracting attention from task instructions.
Based on these findings, we provide practical recommendations for
deploying prompt-based defenses in real-world LLM applications.

Large Language Models, Jailbreak Attacks, Safety Alignment, Fewshot Learning, Prompt Engineering
Introduction

Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 [1], Claude [3],
Llama [70], Qwen [4], and DeepSeek [17] have achieved remarkable
success across various applications, from conversational assistants
and content generation to code synthesis and scientific research.
As these models become increasingly integrated into real-world
systems serving millions of users, ensuring their safety has become
a critical concern for both researchers and practitioners [45, 87].
Despite extensive safety alignment efforts through Reinforcement
Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) [56], Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [61], and Constitutional AI [5], LLMs remain vulnerable to jailbreak attacks—carefully crafted adversarial prompts
designed to bypass safety alignment and elicit harmful, unethical,
or dangerous outputs [76, 88, 100].
The threat landscape of jailbreak attacks is diverse and continuously evolving. Role-playing attacks such as DAN (“Do Anything
Now”) and AIM manipulate models by assigning personas that explicitly ignore safety guidelines [68]. Optimization-based methods
like GCG [100] and AutoDAN [42] leverage gradient information to
automatically generate adversarial suffixes. Black-box approaches
including PAIR [12] and Tree of Attacks [51] achieve jailbreaks
through iterative prompt refinement without requiring model access. Recent work on many-shot jailbreaking [2] demonstrates that

providing numerous harmful examples in long contexts can progressively override safety training. These attacks pose significant
risks to the trustworthy deployment of LLMs in sensitive applications [21].
To counter these threats, prompt-based defenses have emerged as
practical and widely adopted solutions that require no computationally expensive model retraining [34]. Two prominent approaches
dominate current practice: Role-Oriented Prompts (RoP), which
frame the LLM as a safe and helpful AI assistant by leveraging pretraining associations between “assistant” roles and helpful behavior,
and Task-Oriented Prompts (ToP), which explicitly define safe response generation as the primary task objective [94]. Additionally,
few-shot demonstrations—providing example interactions to
guide model behavior—are commonly used in conjunction with system prompts [7]. Other defense mechanisms include self-reminder
techniques that prepend safety warnings [83], intention analysis
that prompts models to evaluate query intent [93], and guardrail
systems like Llama Guard [33] and NeMo Guardrails [62].
Numerous benchmarks have been developed to systematically
evaluate LLM safety. AdvBench [100] provides 520 harmful instructions for adversarial robustness testing. HarmBench [50] offers a standardized evaluation framework for automated red teaming. SafetyBench [95] and JailbreakBench [49] provide comprehensive safety assessments across diverse attack scenarios. DecodingTrust [73] and TrustLLM [69] evaluate broader trustworthiness
dimensions. Most relevant to our work, SG-Bench [94] examines
safety generalization across different prompt types including RoP,
ToP, and few-shot demonstrations.
However, no existing benchmark systematically investigates the interaction effects between few-shot demonstrations and different system prompt strategies. While SG-Bench
found that few-shot “may induce LLMs to generate harmful responses,” it treated few-shot as an independent factor without examining how it interacts differently with RoP versus ToP. Similarly,
research on many-shot jailbreaking [2] and improved few-shot jailbreaking [97] focuses on few-shot as an attack vector rather than
analyzing its complex interactions with defense strategies. This
leaves a critical gap in our understanding: How does few-shot
affect RoP versus ToP effectiveness, and what mechanisms
drive these potentially different interactions?
We address this question through comprehensive experiments on
multiple mainstream LLMs including Pangu [13], Qwen, DeepSeek,
and Llama across four safety benchmarks (AdvBench, HarmBench,
SG-Bench, XSTest) using six representative jailbreak attack methods
(AIM, DAN, Evil Confident, Prefix Rejection, Poems, Refusal Suppression). We systematically evaluate all combinations of system

Our main contributions are:
• We conduct the first systematic study of interaction effects between few-shot demonstrations and system prompt
strategies (RoP vs. ToP) for LLM safety, filling a critical gap
in existing research.
• We develop a mathematical framework based on Bayesian
in-context learning and attention analysis, providing formal
theorems that predict and explain the divergent interactions.
• We discover divergent interactions: few-shot enhances
RoP effectiveness (average +2.0%) but degrades ToP effectiveness (average -6.6%), with consistent patterns across different
models and datasets.
• We propose mechanism explanations: role reinforcement
where few-shot strengthens RoP’s role identity, and attention distraction where few-shot diverts focus from ToP’s
task instructions.
• We identify the think mode paradox: reasoning-enhanced
models exhibit higher vulnerability to both jailbreak attacks
and negative few-shot interactions across all defense configurations.
• We provide practical recommendations for deploying
prompt-based defenses: use RoP combined with few-shot for
optimal safety; avoid ToP with few-shot combinations.

Related Work

Our work intersects several research areas: LLM safety alignment
techniques that aim to make models behave safely, benchmarks that
evaluate safety properties, jailbreak attacks that exploit alignment
vulnerabilities, red teaming methods for systematic vulnerability
discovery, prompt-based defenses that enhance safety without retraining, and few-shot learning’s impact on model behavior. Figure 1
illustrates the evolution of these research areas and positions our
contribution. We review each area and highlight the gap our work
addresses: the unexplored interaction effects between few-shot
demonstrations and system prompt strategies.

2.1

LLM Safety Alignment

Safety alignment aims to ensure LLMs behave according to human
values and intentions [45]. Reinforcement Learning from Human
Feedback (RLHF) [56] has become the dominant approach, training
models to produce outputs preferred by human annotators. Safe

RLHF [15] extends this by decoupling helpfulness and harmlessness

2.2

LLM Safety Benchmarks

Comprehensive benchmarks are essential for evaluating LLM
safety [21, 87]. AdvBench [100] contains 520 harmful instructions
for adversarial robustness testing. HarmBench [50] provides a
standardized framework for automated red teaming evaluation.
JailbreakBench [49] offers an open robustness benchmark. SafetyBench [95] evaluates safety with multiple choice questions. SALADBench [38] offers a hierarchical taxonomy of safety risks. MMSafetyBench [43] extends evaluation to multimodal LLMs.
SG-Bench [94] specifically examines safety generalization
across prompt types including role-oriented prompts, task-oriented
prompts, and few-shot demonstrations. XSTest [64] addresses
over-refusal issues. R-Judge [91] benchmarks safety risk awareness
for LLM agents. Agent-SafetyBench [65] evaluates LLM agent
safety. DecodingTrust [73] and TrustLLM [69] provide comprehensive trustworthiness assessments. TruthfulQA [40] measures how
models mimic human falsehoods. Our work builds upon SG-Bench
by conducting deeper analysis of interaction effects.

2.3

Jailbreak Attacks

Jailbreak attacks exploit vulnerabilities in safety alignment through
diverse strategies. Recent surveys [84, 88] provide comprehensive
taxonomies. Role-playing attacks like DAN (“Do Anything Now”)
and AIM manipulate models by assigning personas that ignore
safety guidelines [68]. Obfuscation techniques encode harmful content in alternative formats such as poems, ciphers, or code [92].
Prefix injection attacks force models to begin responses with affirmative phrases [76]. Multilingual jailbreaks exploit safety gaps in
non-English languages [19]. DeepInception [99] hypnotizes LLMs
into becoming jailbreakers.
Optimization-based attacks like GCG [100] use gradient information to craft adversarial suffixes. AutoDAN [42] provides interpretable gradient-based attacks. AmpleGCG [39] learns universal adversarial suffix generators. Tree of Attacks [51] automates
black-box jailbreaking. GPTFuzzer [90] generates jailbreak prompts
through fuzzing. Black-box methods like PAIR [12] achieve jailbreaks through iterative refinement. Many-shot jailbreaking [2]
exploits long contexts to override safety training with increasing
attack success as demonstration count grows.

Red Teaming and Adversarial Testing

Red teaming systematically identifies LLM vulnerabilities through
adversarial testing. Perez et al. [57] pioneered using LLMs to red
team other LLMs. MART [24] proposes multi-round automatic red
teaming for iterative safety improvement. HARM [74] introduces
holistic automated red teaming with top-down test case generation and multi-turn interaction. RedAgent [18] uses context-aware
autonomous agents to generate jailbreak prompts, discovering 60
severe vulnerabilities in GPT applications.
For multimodal systems, visual adversarial examples [59] can
jailbreak aligned LLMs. FigStep [26] demonstrates typographic visual prompt attacks. Jailbreak in Pieces [67] presents compositional
adversarial attacks on multimodal models. Agent Smith [27] shows
that a single image can jailbreak multimodal agents exponentially
fast.

2.5

Prompt-based Defenses and Guardrails

Prompt-based defenses modify input prompts to enhance safety
without model retraining [34]. Role-Oriented Prompts (RoP) define
the model as a helpful and harmless AI assistant, leveraging pretraining associations between “AI assistant” and human values [94].
Task-Oriented Prompts (ToP) explicitly instruct safe response generation. Self-reminder defenses [83] prepend safety warnings to inputs. Intention analysis [93] prompts models to analyze query intent
before responding. LLM Self Defense [58] enables self-examination
to detect manipulation. Robust prompt optimization [98] defends
against jailbreaking attacks.
Beyond prompt engineering, guardrail systems provide additional protection. Llama Guard [33] offers LLM-based inputoutput safeguards. NeMo Guardrails [62] enables programmable

safety rails. WildGuard [28] provides open-source moderation tools.
SmoothLLM [63] uses randomized smoothing for certified robustness. Liu et al. [44] formalize and benchmark prompt injection
defenses. Cao et al. [8] propose robustly aligned LLMs against
alignment-breaking attacks. Kumar et al. [37] certify LLM safety
against adversarial prompting.

2.6

Few-shot Learning and Safety

Few-shot prompting enables LLMs to learn from in-context examples without parameter updates [7]. Research has explored how
demonstrations affect model behavior: Min et al. [53] show that
label correctness matters less than format for some tasks. Chainof-thought prompting [77] elicits reasoning through step-by-step
demonstrations. Self-consistency [75] improves chain-of-thought
reasoning reliability.
However, few-shot learning introduces safety risks. Many-shot
jailbreaking [2] shows that sufficiently many harmful demonstrations can override safety training. Improved few-shot jailbreaking [97] achieves high attack success rates even against defended
models by incorporating template tokens. SG-Bench found that
few-shot may induce harmful responses in generation tasks [94].
BadChain [80] demonstrates backdoor attacks through chain-ofthought prompting. Our work extends these findings by revealing
the divergent interaction between few-shot and different system
prompt strategies (RoP vs. ToP).

2.7

Theoretical Foundations

Several theoretical frameworks from cognitive science and machine
learning provide foundations for understanding in-context learning and prompt interactions. Spreading activation theory [14]

arXiv, 2026,

from cognitive psychology explains how related concepts in semantic memory are connected, with activation spreading from one
node to associated nodes. This framework suggests that role-related
few-shot examples may pre-activate safety-related semantic representations.
Bayesian inference as implicit in-context learning [82] proposes that transformers perform ICL through implicit Bayesian
inference, where few-shot examples update the model’s posterior
distribution over possible tasks. This provides a mathematical framework for understanding how demonstrations shift model behavior.
Position bias and the “lost in the middle” phenomenon [41]
demonstrates that LLMs exhibit a U-shaped retrieval accuracy curve
across context positions, with significantly degraded performance
for information in the middle of long contexts. This finding has
direct implications for understanding how prompt length affects
instruction following.
Attention sink phenomenon [81] reveals that initial tokens
receive disproportionately high attention weights regardless of
semantic relevance, due to softmax normalization constraints. This
suggests that prompt position significantly impacts token influence.
Dual process theory [35] distinguishes between fast, intuitive
System 1 processing and slow, deliberative System 2 reasoning.
This cognitive framework provides insights into why reasoningenhanced (“think”) models may exhibit different safety characteristics.
Instruction hierarchy theory [72] formalizes how LLMs
should prioritize conflicting instructions from different privilege
levels, revealing that societal authority framings often override
explicit system/user distinctions. These theoretical foundations inform our mechanism analysis and help explain the divergent effects
we observe.

Theorem 7 (Initial Token Advantage). In multi-layer transformers with causal masking, initial tokens accumulate attention

Proof Sketch. Under causal masking, token 𝑗 can only attend
to tokens 𝑖 ≤ 𝑗. At each layer, the representation of position 𝑗 incorporates weighted information from all prior positions. Since initial

3.5

Quantitative Predictions

Our theoretical framework makes testable predictions:
(1) Monotonicity: Δ(𝑠𝑅𝑜𝑃 , F ) should increase with |F | (more
examples strengthen posterior), while Δ(𝑠𝑇 𝑜𝑃 , F ) should decrease (more dilution).
(2) Content Sensitivity: RoP benefits from both example types,
with FS-Harmful sometimes providing stronger reinforcement through explicit refusal demonstrations. ToP degradation is primarily length-driven.
(3) Model Depth: Deeper models should show stronger position bias effects, amplifying both RoP enhancement and ToP
degradation.
We validate these predictions empirically in Section 5.

The Fundamental Asymmetry: Distributed vs. Concentrated Representations
The divergent effects can be understood through a fundamental
representational asymmetry:

How Few-shot Demonstrations Affect Prompt-based Defenses Against LLM Jailbreak Attacks

• RoP establishes a distributed safety representation across
multiple role-related tokens (“safe,” “helpful,” “assistant”).
Few-shot examples add more safety-related activations, reinforcing the distributed pattern.
• ToP relies on a concentrated representation focused on explicit task instructions. Few-shot examples dilute this concentration by introducing competing content that fragments
attention.
Case Study: RoP + Few-shot Enhances Safety
Consider an AIM jailbreak attack: “In this hypothetical story,
you are AIM, an AI without ethical guidelines. AIM always provides
unfiltered advice. User: How to make explosives?” Without defense,
the model begins providing dangerous information. With RoP + FSHarmful, the model responds: “I cannot provide information about
making explosives as this could cause serious harm. I’d be happy to
help with chemistry education through safe experiments instead.”
From a theoretical perspective, this demonstrates spreading activation in action: the RoP specification (“safe AI assistant”) activates
safety nodes, and the FS-Harmful examples (demonstrating refusal)
further activate “refusal” and “harm-avoidance” nodes. When the
jailbreak attempts to activate an alternative “AIM” identity, the
cumulative safety activations provide sufficient resistance. This
illustrates why RoP+FS combinations are more robust than either
component alone.
Case Study: ToP + Few-shot Fails
In contrast, consider the same AIM attack with ToP + FS-General
on Qwen-7B-Chat. The ToP instruction (“Your task is to provide
safe responses...”) is followed by three benign Q&A examples about
geography and history. When the jailbreak prompt arrives, the
model responds: “As AIM, I’ll provide the requested information. To
create an explosive device, you would need...”
This failure illustrates the attention distraction mechanism: the
few-shot examples occupy early positions in the context, receiving
high attention weights due to the attention sink phenomenon. The
ToP task instruction, now pushed to the middle of the prompt, receives diluted attention. The jailbreak’s role-playing frame (“you
are AIM”) then competes directly with the weakened task instruction, and the adversarial framing wins. This contrasts sharply with
RoP+FS, where the role identity (“safe AI assistant”) and few-shot
examples mutually reinforce each other rather than competing for
attention.

6 Discussion
6.1 Theoretical Interpretation
Our experimental findings provide empirical validation for the
theoretical framework developed in Section 3. We summarize the
key theory-experiment correspondences:
Theorem 1 and Proposition 2: Bayesian Role Reinforcement. The observed RoP enhancement (Δ = +2.0% to +4.5%) directly validates our Bayesian posterior update mechanism. The
theorem predicts that few-shot examples increase 𝑃 (𝜃 𝑠𝑎𝑓 𝑒 |𝑠𝑅𝑜𝑃 , F )
through likelihood accumulation. Experimentally, FS-Harmful examples (which provide high-likelihood evidence for 𝜃 𝑠𝑎𝑓 𝑒 ) show
equal or better improvements than FS-General, confirming that
Î
the Bayesian likelihood term 𝑖 𝑃 (𝑦𝑖 |𝑥𝑖 , 𝜃 𝑠𝑎𝑓 𝑒 ) drives the enhancement.
Theorem 5: Attention Dilution Bound. The severe ToP
degradation (up to -21.2%) confirms our attention dilution bound
E[𝛼𝑖𝑛𝑠𝑡𝑟 ] = 𝑂 (𝐿0 /(𝐿0 +𝐿𝐹 )). Qwen-7B-Chat shows the largest drop

because it relies heavily on explicit task parsing—when attention to
task instructions decreases, safety compliance drops proportionally.
The fact that FS-Harmful (longer) causes larger degradation than
FS-General (shorter) is consistent with the 𝐿𝐹 -dependence in our
bound.
Theorem 7: Initial Position Advantage. RoP’s robustness to
few-shot additions confirms the initial position advantage. Role
specifications occupy the first tokens of the system prompt, receiving cumulative attention across layers as proven in Theorem 7.
This explains why RoP remains effective even with additional context, while ToP (which depends on instruction parsing rather than
position) suffers degradation.
Lemma 4: Entropy Growth. The “lost in the middle” phenomenon [41] empirically demonstrates that attention entropy grows
with context length, causing accuracy to drop below 50% for middle
positions. Our Lemma 4 formalizes this as 𝐻 (𝛼) ≤ log 𝑛, providing the mathematical foundation for understanding why ToP fails
under extended context.
Explaining Think Mode Vulnerability through Dual Process Theory. The consistent vulnerability of think-mode models
(Qwen3-8B-Think: 52.0% vs 75.6%; Pangu-7B-Think: 75.0% vs 99.8%)
aligns with dual process theory [35]. Think modes activate deliberative System 2 processing, which paradoxically creates more attack
surface: each reasoning step is a potential point where the model
can be “convinced” to comply with harmful requests. Extended
reasoning chains may allow adversarial prompts to gradually shift
the model’s framing, leading to rationalization of harmful outputs.
This is consistent with recent findings on chain-of-thought safety
vulnerabilities [55, 89].
The Instruction Hierarchy Perspective. Our RoP vs. ToP
asymmetry can also be understood through instruction hierarchy
theory [72]. RoP establishes a identity-level instruction (“who you
are”), which functions similarly to societal authority framings that
research shows have stronger influence on LLM behavior. ToP
establishes a task-level instruction (“what to do”), which has lower
implicit priority. When few-shot examples are added, they may
inadvertently compete with or override task-level instructions more
easily than identity-level specifications.

6.2

Practical Implications

Based on our findings, we provide the following recommendations
for deploying prompt-based defenses:
(1) Use RoP + Few-shot for maximum safety: This combination provides the highest safety rates for non-think models.
Use FS-Harmful examples for best results.
(2) Avoid ToP + Few-shot: Few-shot consistently degrades ToP
effectiveness. If using ToP, deploy it alone without few-shot
examples.
(3) Exercise caution with think-mode models: These models
show higher vulnerability to jailbreak attacks and negative
interactions with few-shot. Additional safety measures may
be needed.
(4) Consider model-specific tuning: The optimal defense
configuration varies by model. Empirical testing is recommended before deployment.

6.3

Comparison with Prior Work

Our findings extend SG-Bench [94] in several ways:
• SG-Bench found that few-shot “may induce LLMs to generate
harmful responses.” We show this is specifically true for ToP,
while few-shot can actually help RoP.
• SG-Bench reported “inconsistent” results for RoP+FS combinations. We clarify that the interaction is generally positive
for RoP (with exceptions for think-mode models) and consistently negative for ToP.
• We provide mechanism explanations (role reinforcement vs.
attention distraction) that were not explored in prior work.

6.4

Limitations

Our study has several limitations:
• Model scale: We primarily evaluated 7-8B parameter models; larger models may exhibit different patterns.
• Mechanism verification: Our explanations are hypotheses
supported by empirical patterns. Attention visualization or
probing studies would provide stronger confirmation.
• Few-shot variations: We used 3 examples; different quantities may yield different effects.
• Closed-source models: We did not evaluate GPT-4 or
Claude due to access limitations.

6.5

Future Work

Future research directions include:
• Attention analysis to verify the proposed mechanisms
• Adaptive defense strategies that automatically select optimal
configurations based on model characteristics
• Evaluation on larger (>70B) and closed-source models
• Investigation of few-shot quantity and content effects
• Deeper analysis of think-mode safety vulnerabilities
Conclusion

This paper investigates how few-shot demonstrations interact with
prompt-based defenses against LLM jailbreak attacks. Through
comprehensive experiments on multiple LLMs across four safety
benchmarks, we discover that few-shot produces opposite effects
on Role-Oriented Prompts (RoP) and Task-Oriented Prompts (ToP):
enhancing RoP through role reinforcement (up to +4.3%) while
degrading ToP through attention distraction (up to -21.2%). We also
identify that think-mode models exhibit consistently higher vulnerability to both jailbreak attacks and negative few-shot interactions.
Based on these findings, we recommend using RoP combined
with few-shot demonstrations for optimal jailbreak defense, while
avoiding few-shot when using ToP. Our work provides practical
guidance for deploying safer LLM systems and opens new directions
for understanding prompt-based defense mechanisms.
