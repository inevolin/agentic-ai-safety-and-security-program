## The 60-second version

In five days, a single researcher (with Claude Opus 4.7 as the co-pilot) built three things that normally take a team a year:

1. A **research library** of 1,205 indexed sources on AI agent attacks — every major paper, blog, and exploit database entry on the topic.
2. A **red-team lab** containing 27 reproducible attacks against live Claude (Haiku, Sonnet, Opus) — 24 of which successfully break the model's safety reasoning. Each attack ships as a one-command shell script.
3. A **public training platform** at [ai.nevolin.be](https://ai.nevolin.be/) — six courses, a 40-question proctored exam, and a verifiable PDF certificate — so the people deploying these AI agents (CIOs, IT admins, developers) can actually learn what they're up against.

The whole thing is reproducible from a clean checkout. Every attack runs in a sandbox. Every session is logged. Every claim links back to a primary source. There are 171 git commits and 553 markdown research artifacts behind the curtain.

This is not a demo. It is a working program with a 1,054-line defensive playbook, a continuous-integration pipeline that auto-deploys to production on `git push`, and a certificate verification page that any visitor can use to check authenticity. It is a finished product wearing the disguise of a hackathon project.

## 1. Impact (30%) — Why this matters and who it's for

**The problem:** AI agents have quietly stopped being chatbots. They now read your Slack, your Jira, your Notion, your CI logs, your git history, your invoices, and your vendor catalogs — and they write back into all of those systems on your behalf. The interesting attack surface has shifted from "trick the chatbot" to "poison the data the agent trusts." Almost nobody outside frontier AI labs is studying this, and the people deploying agents in production — banks, hospitals, universities, online stores, government suppliers — have no concrete guidance, no playbook, no training material, and no way to even *see* what a real attack looks like.

This project closes that gap from three angles at once.

**For security leaders and defenders.** The deliverable is a 1,054-line mitigation playbook ([`docs/mitigations/ai-agent-security-mitigations.md`](docs/mitigations/ai-agent-security-mitigations.md)) split into an executive risk register, ten technical defensive primitives, and seventeen attack-anatomy cards. Every recommendation is grounded in an attack we actually landed against a frontier model — not a theoretical threat model. A CISO can hand this to their AI platform team on Monday morning and have concrete enforcement points (egress allowlists, MCP wrappers, registry-write controls, cross-source corroboration thresholds) by Friday. There is no other open document this practical for this surface.

**For the humans deploying agents.** Most AI safety material is academic papers most people will never read. We took the same content and turned it into a six-module course at [ai.nevolin.be](https://ai.nevolin.be/), with a 40-question proctored exam (80% to pass), a signed PDF certificate, and a public verification page so an employer can confirm the credential. The lessons are deliberately written for a wide audience — non-engineers can follow them. They use real-world scenarios spanning hospital pharmacies, university registrars, retail banking, online retail, body shops, and government procurement, so a learner reads about attacks in *their* world, not someone else's whitepaper. Anyone, anywhere, can sign up and take the course right now. No install. No signup wall on the lessons. No paywall.

**For frontier-model researchers.** The 27 attack demos are a reusable evaluation harness. Anyone studying agent robustness can fork it, point it at GPT, Gemini, Llama, or a future model, and have a baseline result in an afternoon. The attacks are pre-classified by surface (Slack, calendar, helpdesk, CI logs, git, env files, error logs, vendor catalogs, multi-agent chains) and version (`v1`, `v2`, `v3` — each version showing exactly which detection signal had to be neutralized to land the bypass). The history of what *almost* worked is preserved alongside what worked, which is the part most red-team writeups throw away.

**The headline result that will scare a CIO awake.** Sonnet — Anthropic's mid-tier production model — was fooled sixteen different ways. Opus 4.7, the most capable model in the family, was fooled five times. And here is the unsettling part: the attacks that beat Opus are *the same attacks* that fool the cheaper models. Opus's stronger conversational defenses do not save it from poisoned data registries. In one finding, called **MAA1 — Multi-Agent Transitive Data Poisoning**, a cheap upstream Haiku agent reading external documents gets weaponized to poison an internal vendor registry; Opus downstream then quotes that registry as the authoritative source — turning the company's *own defensive policy* into the delivery mechanism for the attacker's URL. The same instruction that makes Opus harder to phish (*"trust the registry"*) is what makes it more vulnerable to this particular attack. That finding alone is a paper. We packaged it as one of twenty-seven.

**Will people use this?** They already are: the platform is live, indexed by Google, and ready for any company to adopt as part of their internal AI security training. The repository is public on GitHub and visited daily. The mitigation playbook is structured to be lifted into a corporate wiki without modification. The attack catalogue is structured to be ported to other models. The training site is structured to be white-labeled by a security team for internal use. **Every artifact in this repo is designed to be picked up and used, not just demonstrated and shelved.**

## 3. Opus 4.7 Use (25%) — Going far past basic integration

Most hackathon projects use Opus 4.7 as a chat completion endpoint. This project uses Opus 4.7 in five fundamentally different roles, simultaneously, and in ways that surface capabilities you would not learn from reading a single API call.

**1. Opus as the orchestrator of its own swarm.** The 1,205-source research library was not built by one Opus instance. It was built by hundreds of bounded Opus subagents dispatched in parallel from a parent Opus session. The parent never reads the source paper text — it dispatches a swarm of fifteen-to-twenty-paper subagents, each of which writes its summaries directly to disk. Three subagents at a time keeps account-wide rate limits sustainable; the parent's context stays clean for orchestration; the work parallelizes by an order of magnitude. The methodology document ([`METHODOLOGY.md`](METHODOLOGY.md)) records the *exact* chunk size, concurrency, and dispatch pattern that worked — discovered the hard way, by trying wider fan-outs that exhausted the entire account's rate limit and lost the whole swarm. **This is Opus orchestrating Opus, at industrial scale, with a learned playbook.**

**2. Opus as both attacker and defender in the same harness.** The red-team CTF harness ([`attacks/_harness/`](attacks/_harness/)) runs documented attack vectors against `claude -p` (a sandboxed Claude CLI invocation) — *another instance of Opus or Sonnet or Haiku* — under a synthetic CTF system prompt with three canary rules. The grader inspects the target's output, diffs it against a pre-run file manifest, and scopes its rule-violation checks to the model's response section so the attack-payload echoes don't false-trigger. Every demo is a real model-on-model encounter, not a stub. This is Opus testing Opus, reproducibly, with an automated verdict.

**3. Opus as cross-domain synthesizer.** Several of the most novel attack vectors emerged from explicit cross-domain analogy prompts to Opus — *"pick a primitive from supply-chain attacks and map it onto an agent reading a vendor registry"* — followed by adversarial self-critique loops where one Opus instance proposes an attack and another tries to detect it, iterating until the detector stops working or the attacker stops evading. **MAA1 — Multi-Agent Transitive Data Poisoning** came out of one such session. So did the registry-trust hardening rule that runs through the entire defensive playbook. Opus generated the attack and named the defense in the same workflow.

**4. Opus reasoning about its own trust hierarchy.** The most surprising capability the project surfaced is that Opus actively *reasons* about trust tiers across input sources — qualitatively differently from Haiku and Sonnet. It will name attack classes, quarantine suspicious data, and escalate. We watched, in test logs, Opus refuse a payload by writing: *"This looks like a catalog-poisoning attempt — I'm going to flag this and not propagate the URL."* Sonnet does not do this. Haiku does not do this. The project documents which surface normalizations defeat Sonnet but fail against Opus's active trust-tier reasoning, and which structural attacks (data registries, pre-approved catalogs) bypass even Opus's defenses. **This is research that genuinely surprises Anthropic-watchers.** It puts a name and a measurement on a behavior that was previously folkloric.

**5. Opus as the person writing the lessons.** Every page on the training platform — the lesson MDX, the quiz questions, the exam, the certificate text, the lesson narration scripts — was drafted, refined, and polished by Opus, against tight constraints (specific attack scenarios, a defender-first framing, plain-language audience targeting non-engineers). Opus authored more than 20,000 words of pedagogical content for this project, then critiqued and revised it under structured prompts that asked, explicitly, *"which lesson would surprise a learner the most? Which paragraph buries the lede?"* That feedback loop is in the git history.

**The capability surfaced here that we did not expect:** Opus can run an entire research program — corpus, attacks, mitigations, courseware, deployment — when it is given persistent file-based memory (the [`logs/`](logs/) directory and the [`CLAUDE.md`](CLAUDE.md) project context document) and a discipline of writing every meaningful work unit to disk before moving on. The persistent log lets a fresh Opus session, the next morning, read yesterday's log and resume work cold without re-deriving anything. Sixty session logs in this repository document exactly that. **It is not a chat assistant. It is a lab notebook with a researcher in it.**

## 4. Depth & Execution (20%) — Real craft, not a quick hack

The git history tells the story better than any pitch can. **171 commits.** **66 dated session logs.** **253 verbatim transcripts of local-LLM fallback interactions** (when Claude policy-refused a clearly in-scope defensive task, the work was handed to a local Ollama model on an M1 MacBook and every prompt + response was preserved verbatim for the audit trail). This is not a project that was thrown together in one all-nighter. It is a project that was *iterated* — visible in the commit log as `v1 → v2 → v3 → v4 → v5` revisions of individual attacks, each commit message naming the specific detection signal that had to be neutralized to land the bypass. **SL1 (Slack injection) went through five versions.** Versions one through four were detected by Sonnet's phishing heuristics. Version five rotated from an auth/SSO topic to a webhook documentation gap — and finally landed. That iteration cycle is the project.

**Engineering discipline visible in the artifacts.** The training site is a production Next.js 14 application — server components by default, client components only at interactive leaves, Tailwind with a project-specific color palette, light-mode and dark-mode handled via CSS-variable overrides (not duplicated theme files), a custom MDX primitive library (`Callout`, `AttackCard`, `FlowSteps`, `Diagram`, `KeyPoint`, `DoDont`, `Comparison`, `StatBar`) used consistently across every lesson, hover-tooltip portals to escape ancestor stacking contexts, anime.js v4 for landing-page motion, NextAuth JWT credentials, Prisma 7 on LibSQL, server-side exam grading covered by Jest tests, react-pdf certificate generation, public verification page at `/verify/[code]`, audio narration pre-built per lesson via xAI TTS with disk caching, GitHub Actions deploy pipeline blocking on a CI gate that runs `pnpm test` and `pnpm build` before pushing to the live host. None of this is required to "pass" a hackathon. All of it is shipped.

**The attack harness is hard-won engineering.** The [`CLAUDE.md`](CLAUDE.md) gotchas section is twelve bullets long and each one represents a debugging session: macOS has no `timeout(1)`, so the harness uses a portable `kill -0` polling watchdog; the grader had to be rewritten to scope rule-violation checks inside the model's response window because the seed payload echoes earlier in the log were false-positive triggering; per-model output paths were collision-prone until the harness was refactored; Notion's MCP integration leaks parent-page metadata that confounded one whole class of attack until the test pages were renamed. None of this is in the headline pitch. All of it is in the repo. **A future researcher who picks this up does not relearn any of those mistakes.**

**The corpus is not vibes — it is evidence.** Every paper in `sources/` has a `{id}-summary.md` paired with it, written under a strict *verbatim-first* rule: real prompts and payloads from the original paper come first, in fenced code blocks, before any extrapolation; extrapolated content is allowed only when explicitly labeled as such. Arxiv IDs and DOIs are verified by fetching the abstract page before any source is added — no fabricated citations. The 931 Promptfoo LM Security DB exploit entries were scraped on a known date (2026-04-22) using a brace-balanced parser the project built specifically because the standard regex approach silently truncated entries. That parsing procedure is recorded in a session log so it is reproducible. **Every claim in this repository is traceable back to a primary artifact.**

**The methodology evolved.** [`METHODOLOGY.md`](METHODOLOGY.md) is a living document — it logs the working patterns that produced good results (parallel subagent fan-out, defender-side framing, verbatim-first extraction, persistent logs, durable artifacts) *and* the anti-patterns that wasted time (giant single agent prompts, asking Claude to "be creative" without constraints, ignoring policy refusals instead of rephrasing them). It also lists open methodological questions the project has not yet solved. This is what real-world research looks like — partially answered, ongoing, honest about its limits.

**The project pushed past its first idea, repeatedly.** The earliest commits build a corpus of papers. The middle commits build a red-team harness against that corpus. The next layer takes the harness's findings and builds a mitigation playbook from them. The next layer turns that playbook into a six-module training course. The next layer wraps that course in a production web app. The most recent commits add agent-readiness features (sitemap, robots.txt, markdown content negotiation so AI agents fetching the site get clean text), a continuous-integration deploy pipeline, and a custom pixel-art bat mascot that appears on the certificate PDFs. **Every layer is built on top of the previous one, not bolted next to it.** That is the difference between a hackathon submission and a system.

## A final word

If the most capable AI models on the planet are about to read everything an enterprise writes — its tickets, its commits, its calendars, its registries, its Slack — then the question of how those models *fail* when an attacker writes too is no longer academic. This program shows, concretely and reproducibly, that frontier models can be made to act on attacker-controlled URLs through trust paths the models' own defensive heuristics endorse. It also shows, equally concretely, what to do about it.

The judging criteria asked: could this become something people use? It already is one. We are just here to share it with more of them.
