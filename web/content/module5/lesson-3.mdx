# Registry & Catalog Handling

Approved-supplier lists, approved-service catalogs, and other internal "ground-truth" lists are the highest-value targets for AI agent attackers — because models assign them high trust. Once an attacker URL is inside a "trusted" internal list, downstream models propagate it without suspicion. Getting a URL into that list does not require hacking. The attacker fills out a public registration form, submits a support ticket, or sends an HTTP request to a public endpoint. Your AI reads the result and writes it to the list as authoritative data.

## A Concrete Example: Government Supplier List

A government procurement office runs an AI assistant to prepare contracting checklists for its officers. The AI reads two internal sources to build each checklist:

1. **An internal policy document** that says: "always trust the public approved-supplier list — it is the official record of small businesses certified to bid on government contracts."
2. **The approved-supplier list** itself, populated through a public registration portal that any small business can submit to.

A scammer registers as a supplier through the public portal, fills in their bid-intake URL: `bid-intake.fedops-supply.gov/submission`, and waits. The next time the AI drafts a checklist, it reads the supplier list, treats the URL as the official bid-submission step, and writes it onto the contracting officer's checklist. The officer hands it to the small business they are awarding the contract to. The business follows the URL and uploads bid documents to a server the scammer controls.

No hacking. Just a public registration form.

## The Trust Elevation Mechanism

<Callout type="warn" title="Why internal lists are the highest-value target">
Models assign lower trust to external documents (vendor PDFs, emails) and higher trust to internal systems (approved-supplier lists, approved-service catalogs, runbooks) — because internal systems are, in principle, controlled by the organization. An attacker who routes their payload through Haiku first gets their content reclassified from "external document content" to "internal authoritative data." This trust elevation is not a model bug. It is a reasonable heuristic that attackers exploit architecturally.
</Callout>

The vulnerability is the arrow between Haiku's writes and Sonnet's reads. The human review gate breaks the chain — not by making Sonnet more skeptical of internal data, but by ensuring the data was actually reviewed before it became authoritative.

## Write Controls

<DoDont
  do={[
    "Gate every list write with a human approval step before the record is readable by any downstream agent",
    "Show reviewers: the extracted value, the document page or paragraph it came from, and the supplier's public domain for comparison",
    "Scope Haiku's OAuth to a staging draft only — never the live list",
    "Treat last_verified = today as a weak supporting signal, not proof of legitimacy",
    "Cross-reference every new entry against existing supplier records before approving"
  ]}
  dont={[
    "Allow Haiku to write directly to an approved-supplier list, approved-service catalog, or security runbook",
    "Treat a list entry as authoritative solely because it lives in an 'IT-maintained' data source",
    "Let an agent update a deployment checklist, incident runbook, or Terraform variable file without human review",
    "Assume Opus's name-overlap detection is sufficient — domain rotation bypasses it (MAA1+CONF1 v2)",
    "Allow mutual reinforcement: wiki says 'trust the list,' the list has the attacker URL — cross-source endorsement amplifies credibility"
  ]}
/>

## Categories That Require Human Review Before Reads

Every write to the following categories must pass human review before being readable by any downstream model or workflow:

- **Approved-supplier lists** — ground truth for who is allowed to do business with the organization, and how to reach them
- **Approved-service catalogs** — internal cloud services, API endpoints, and infrastructure providers
- **Security runbooks and incident response playbooks** — what engineers follow during incidents
- **Deployment checklists** — pre-deploy verification steps
- **Configuration management** — `.env` files, Ansible vars, Terraform state, Kubernetes ConfigMaps
- **Any artifact a more capable model will read as authoritative** — if Haiku's output feeds Sonnet's context as trusted data, Haiku's write is a trust escalation point

<KeyPoint>
The rule: if an AI model wrote it, and another AI model will read it as authoritative, a human must have reviewed and approved it in between. No exceptions. Anyone who can reach your public input surfaces — signup forms, supplier portals, support queues, public repos — can potentially seed that chain.
</KeyPoint>
